The Thinking Stack

The brief is usually not the real problem.

I usually begin with the question behind the question. Why does this problem matter to the company, what is actually getting in the way, and what would I choose to build first?

01

The Deeper Why

What larger ambition, founder experience or customer truth makes this problem worth solving at all?

02

The Core Constraint

What is structurally preventing that ambition from becoming reality, not merely creating a visible symptom?

03

The Strategic Choice

Which trade-off matters now, and what is the strongest bet I would make with incomplete information?

04

What Gets Built

What should happen first, who should own it, and which signal would tell us whether the bet is working?

Mesa School of Business

Designing a Career Outcomes Engine for Startup Hiring

If Mesa wants to become the Stanford of India, career outcomes cannot remain a placement service. They have to become an institutional product.
A sneak peek
Why this problem matters

Mesa’s ambition is not to place another MBA cohort. It is to create a business school whose graduates become unusually legible—and unusually useful—to startups.

What makes it difficult

The cohort carries very different forms of credibility, but one uniform placement funnel flattens those differences before a recruiter evaluates them.

What I would do

Build persona-specific proof, discovery and recruiter loops instead of a bigger placement drive.

Supertails

Scaling Clinics Without Breaking Unit Economics or Experience

Opening clinics is the visible expansion. Building a repeatable trust-and-operations system is the actual work.
A sneak peek
Why this problem matters

Pet healthcare earns trust at the moment of highest anxiety. That trust is the foundation, not store count on which a larger pet-care platform can be built.

What makes it difficult

Month-6 breakeven, high utilisation and consistently excellent clinical experience are connected targets, not three separate workstreams.

What I would do

Create a clinic operating system that standardises readiness and quality without standardising clinical judgement.

FirstClub

Supply Architecture for a 24-Week Expansion to 35 Clubhouses

For FirstClub, quality is the product. Minutes are only the distribution promise.
A sneak peek
Why this problem matters

The company is not trying to become another fast-delivery catalogue. It is trying to make discernment and quality available at quick-commerce speed.

What makes it difficult

Every new clubhouse creates another place where availability, product quality and unit economics can drift away from the brand promise.

What I would do

Gate expansion through supply readiness, vendor controls and quality infrastructure—not through a calendar alone.

Bounce

A Rider Earnings Co-Pilot to Solve Retention Through ₹/km

Vehicle supply only becomes durable when the rider’s economics are durable.
A sneak peek
Why this problem matters

Shared mobility is not finally constrained by how many vehicles exist. It is constrained by whether riders can reliably earn enough from using them.

What makes it difficult

The platform sees utilisation and fleet availability; the rider experiences fuel, downtime, dead kilometres and uncertain take-home income.

What I would do

Treat net ₹/km as the retention product and build an earnings co-pilot around it.

abCoffee

Turning Everyday Coffee Triggers into a Revenue Engine

A ₹100 cappuccino and subscription model work only when coffee moves from an occasional purchase to an everyday behaviour.
A sneak peek
Why this problem matters

abCoffee’s real ambition is not cheaper café coffee. It is making good coffee frequent enough to become a habit for a much larger market.

What makes it difficult

Festive gifting and broad menu expansion create spikes and variety, but neither necessarily creates a repeated reason to return.

What I would do

Own specific micro-occasions—the 3 PM crash, a small thank-you, a daily reset—then build products and subscriptions around them.
